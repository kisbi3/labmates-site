01 · What the lab does

A lab, not a chatbot

  1. Second BrainNotes that remember each other.

    Your Markdown notes are scanned into a research knowledge graph — papers, concepts, methods, claims. Ask a question in plain language and the lab answers from your own notes with sources, falling back to the raw PDFs when the notes run out. The index is derived and deletable; your files are never rewritten.

  2. WorkbenchWhere reading and running meet.

    One editor for the whole research loop: Markdown with equations and cross-references, code with full syntax support, notebooks running on local or SSH Jupyter kernels, terminals, and a git workbench with commit graph and staging. Obsidian-style canvas boards, frontmatter table views, and voice memos live in the same vault.

  3. Day tierDirect the science in conversation.

    One orchestrator role with two bodies — a Claude Code session at your desk and a Telegram coordinator in your pocket — sharing one memory. You talk to whichever is closer; it routes work to project professors and their graduate-student, reviewer, and validator agents.

  4. Night labPreparation while you sleep.

    Opt-in night workers run the unglamorous upstream: literature radar, inbox staging, reproduction attempts, cross-project connection scans. Everything they produce wakes up as an unratified candidate in your morning briefing — never as a silent change to your research record.

  5. ConnectionsThe lab notices what you would have missed.

    The one seat that sees all your projects at once scans for candidate links between them — a method in project A that fits an open problem in project B. Professors grade the evidence; you accept or reject each edge in a short morning conversation.

  6. GatesValidation with no conflict of interest.

    A result passes three separate questions: a validator reruns it against criteria you locked in advance, a professor judges whether it matters, and you decide whether it enters the record. The author never stamps its own work, and the validator runs on a different model than the author.

02 · How it earns trust

Agents prepare. You ratify.

  1. 1 No agent holds terminal authority.Every agent output is a proposal. Promotion into the research record happens only in a conversation with you, through explicit confirmation phrases the agent cannot forge.
  2. 2 Author is never validator.Validation reruns the work on an independent model against criteria you locked before the work started. Nobody grades their own homework.
  3. 3 Unpublished science stays home.Memory is layered: free third-party night models see operational pointers only, never raw results. Full scientific truth lives in project files that only trusted, local seats can read.
  4. 4 Markdown is canonical.The graph, the search index, and every AI artifact live in a derived folder you can delete at any time. Your original notes are never modified by the system.
  5. 5 The workbench reads; the gate writes.The desktop app opens lab state strictly read-only. Any state change — even one proposed by an agent in chat — passes a preview, an exact server-issued token, and your explicit confirmation.
  6. 6 Nights are local-first.Unattended work prefers local models: near-zero cost, private by construction, and free to run all night. Cloud models are the fallback, not the default.

04 · FAQ

The questions researchers ask

How is this different from Obsidian with an AI plugin?
Obsidian graphs your notes; LabMates also staffs them. The same vault that feeds the knowledge graph feeds a working lab — agents that search literature, rerun analyses, and queue findings for your judgment — with an authority model deciding what they may and may not touch.
Can the agents change my research record?
No. Agent output lands as unratified candidates on task boards. Claims, decisions, and canonical notes change only when you promote them through an explicit confirmation the agent cannot issue itself.
What leaves my machine?
Day-tier model calls go through your own Claude or Codex subscription. Night work prefers local models; when free third-party models are used, they receive operational summaries and pointers — never raw results or unpublished values.
What do I need to run it?
A folder of Markdown notes and a Claude Code or Codex subscription for the day tier. The installer bundles everything else — its own Python runtime and the agent layer — into one directory, without touching your system Python.
Am I locked in?
Your vault is plain Markdown folders, readable by any editor including Obsidian. Delete the derived index folder and not one byte of your notes changes.
When can I use it?
LabMates is in private beta with hand-distributed builds. Join the waitlist and we will write to you when public builds are signed and ready.
